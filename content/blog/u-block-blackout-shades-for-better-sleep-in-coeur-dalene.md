<hr /><h2><strong>1. Beyond Darkness: Understanding the Science of Sleep and Light</strong></h2>
<p>It’s no secret that a dark room is essential for good sleep, but do you know why? Our bodies operate on a powerful internal clock called the <strong>circadian rhythm</strong>. This rhythm is profoundly influenced by light exposure. When our eyes detect light, especially blue light, our brains interpret it as daytime, suppressing the production of <strong>melatonin</strong>, the hormone critical for initiating and maintaining sleep.</p>
<p>Even small amounts of light—from streetlights, car headlights, or a sliver under traditional curtains—can disrupt melatonin production, leading to:</p>
<ul><li>Difficulty falling asleep.</li>
<li>Frequent awakenings during the night.</li>
<li>Less restorative deep sleep.</li>
<li>Feeling groggy and unrefreshed in the morning.</li>
</ul>
<p>In Northern Idaho, where summer evenings linger late and bright mornings arrive early, achieving true darkness for optimal sleep is a necessity.</p>
